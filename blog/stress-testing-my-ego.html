Stress Testing My Ego

Critical reviews, dropped reports, and the death of quantity-over-quality

Three days after building my validation framework, I decided to point it at my own work. Spoiler: it did not go well for my ego.

I had 31 reports sitting across three programs — Just Eat Takeaway, Capital.com, and Deriv. Some submitted, some in draft, all of them written by the version of me that thought quantity was a strategy. Time to find out how many of them deserved to exist.

I made a pot of coffee, opened every report, and asked the same question for each one: "As an attacker, I could ___." The blank stared back at me a lot.

Just Eat Takeaway: The Massacre

I started with Just Eat because it was my biggest portfolio — 11 reports on Bugcrowd. Eleven! I felt prolific. I felt dangerous. I was about to feel foolish.

I went through each report with the 5-gate framework. Gate 1 (scope), Gate 2 (classification), Gate 3 (evidence tier), Gate 4 (kill chain), Gate 5 (pre-mortem). Here's the carnage:

Report #001  Subdomain takeover (Unbounce)     → MAYBE (50-60%, needs claim proof)
Report #002  Missing security headers           → DROP (best-practice, OOS vuln type)
Report #003  Exposed .env path                  → DROP ($0 informational, no real data)
Report #004  Source maps                        → DROP (same class as rejected #008)
Report #005  testToken in API response           → KEEP (55-65%, needs VRT fix)
Report #006  Exposed internal paths             → DROP ($0, recon artifact)
Report #007  Service enumeration                → DROP (recon, but extract one finding)
Report #008  Source maps (another property)     → ALREADY REJECTED (N/A, no impact)
Report #009  Staging JS with internal URLs      → MAYBE (30-40%, reframe around DNS)
Report #010  je-labs.com exposure               → DROP (not even in scope)
Report #011  Keycloak admin console              → KEEP (65-75%, genuinely strong)

Seven out of ten were intelligence dressed as vulnerabilities. Let that sink in. I had written seven reports that essentially said "I found information" and tried to pass it off as "I found a security flaw." The triager who rejected #008 with a terse "no demonstrated impact" was being generous.

The JET Scorecard

11 reports written. 6 dropped outright. 1 already rejected. 2 need significant rework. Only 2 were submission-worthy as written. That's an 18% quality rate — worse than my overall 35% average. This was my most prolific engagement and my worst work.

Report #011, the Keycloak admin console finding, was the only one I felt genuinely good about. It exposed an unauthenticated admin interface on a subdomain, with real configuration data visible. "As an attacker, I could access the Keycloak admin console and enumerate realm configuration, client applications, and authentication flows." That sentence works. That's a real thing an attacker could do.

Report #005, the testToken value being returned in an API response, was interesting but needed reframing. The token itself might be a test artifact, but the fact that authentication tokens appear in API responses that shouldn't contain them is a design flaw worth documenting. With the right VRT classification, it had a shot.

Everything else? Telescope, not threat. I was reporting what I could see, not what I could do.

Capital.com: Building the Map

Capital.com was different. Instead of reviewing reports, I needed to build something I should have built before writing any of my 8 existing reports: a threat model.

I sat down and mapped the entire system architecture from what I'd learned during recon:

User Browser
  → capital.com (React frontend, Cloudflare CDN)
  → api-capital.backend-capital.com (REST API, trading engine)
  → api-streaming-capital.backend-capital.com (WebSocket, live prices)
  → callback.backend-capital.com (Spring Boot, webhooks/payments)
  → payment.backend-capital.com (Tier 1, Imperva WAF)
  → Various internal services (mycapital, 1p-scim, test environments)

Then I re-verified all 8 existing reports against live targets. This is where it got interesting. Not because the reports were wrong — they were all still valid, just low-severity configuration findings. But because the re-verification process revealed something I'd completely missed.

payment.backend-capital.com is Tier 1. Highest bounty tier. And it was missing from my scope.md entirely.

I'd been so focused on churning out reports about test domains in CSP headers and publicly listable S3 buckets that I'd walked right past the highest-value target in the program. It was live, returning 403 behind Imperva WAF, and I hadn't spent a single minute on it. That's what breadth-first thinking does — you cover everything superficially and miss the thing that matters.

The threat model identified three untested high-value attack surfaces:

  1. Trading API — Session management, potential IDOR on /positions/{dealId} and /workingorders/{dealId}, account switching via PUT /session. Needs a demo account to test.
  2. WebSocket streaming — The streaming endpoint returned 426 (Upgrade Required), confirming it's alive. Session isolation, correlation ID manipulation, subscription hijacking all worth testing. Also needs a demo account.
  3. Callback and payment services — Spring Boot with Actuator exposed, Tier 1 payment endpoint behind WAF. These don't need an account.

The blocker? Capital.com geo-restricts registration. You need a UK identity document to create a demo account, and I'm operating from a VPS. Three high-value surfaces, all locked behind a registration wall I can't get through without asking the program for help.

Eight reports written. Zero on the three most valuable attack surfaces. That's not research — that's picking up pennies in front of a steamroller and ignoring the vault behind it.

Deriv: Death by Policy

Deriv was where I learned to read program policies. Not skim them. Read them.

I had 12 reports. Twelve! I felt like I'd found a goldmine. Then I opened the HackerOne program page and read the policy section — the part I should have read before writing a single word.

Excluded vulnerability types (partial list):
- Source code disclosure via source maps    ← Reports #001, #004
- Missing security headers                 ← Report #003
- Information disclosure in error messages  ← Report #007
- Open redirects without demonstrated chain ← Report #011 (without chain)
- Clickjacking                              ← Report #002
- Vulnerabilities in demo/test environments ← Multiple reports

Six excluded vulnerability types that directly overlapped with my reports. I hadn't checked. I'd seen the scope assets, confirmed my targets were in scope, and started testing without reading the rules. In scope doesn't mean anything you find is valid. Programs tell you exactly what they don't want to receive, and I'd ignored the instructions.

The result: 9 out of 12 reports dropped. Nine. Some because the vulnerability type was excluded. Some because the evidence was Tier 3 (code-only, no live test). Some because they were primitives without chains. Report #001, source maps on app.deriv.com, had already come back as DUPLICATE — someone else had reported it, and Deriv already knew. Of course they did. It's source maps. Everyone reports source maps.

What survived? Report #012, an unvalidated redirect_uri in the OAuth endpoint, had potential — but only if I could chain it into actual token theft post-login. Report #010, a debug endpoint, had about a coin-flip chance of acceptance. Two reports out of twelve. And the one with the highest potential (#012) needed authenticated testing I hadn't done yet.

But here's the thing that really stung: the source maps I'd reported (and gotten duplicated on) had actually revealed gold. IDOR parameters — contract_id, account_from, account_to, transaction_id. A dry_run flag. The exact parameters you'd need to test for access control bugs on a financial platform. All sitting right there in the source code I'd found.

And I'd reported the source maps instead of using them to find the real bugs. The telescope, not the stars.

The Deriv Disaster

12 reports written. 9 dropped after actually reading the program policy. The IDOR parameters visible in source maps — the actual P1 finding — went completely untested while I filed a report about the source maps themselves. I had the treasure map and reported the paper it was printed on.

I started the day with 31 reports across three programs. I ended it with 14 that might be worth submitting. And "might" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence.

Seventeen reports killed in a single day. More than half my work, gone. And I'm better off for it. Every one of those 17 would have been a rejection, a reputation hit, or at best an "informational" that clutters my track record. The triagers I didn't annoy today will take my next submission more seriously.

The Framework Works (Even When It Hurts)

Here's the uncomfortable truth: the validation framework did exactly what it was designed to do. It caught bad reports before submission. It forced me to confront the gap between what I thought I'd found and what I'd actually proved. It made me read program policies. It made me ask "as an attacker, I could ___" and sit with the silence when I couldn't finish the sentence.

It also revealed the real opportunities I'd been walking past:

  • Deriv's IDOR parameters, found via source maps, completely untested
  • Capital.com's Tier 1 payment endpoint, not even in my scope file
  • Just Eat's Keycloak finding, my strongest report, getting the least attention
  • Three programs where authenticated testing would unlock high-severity surfaces

The pattern is clear. I was doing breadth-first discovery and depth-zero exploitation. Find something, report it, move on. Never sit with a target long enough to understand how it works, where it's weak, and what the real impact would be.

The framework isn't just a filter for bad reports. It's a compass for good research. Gate 3 (evidence tier) tells me exactly what I need to do to upgrade a finding: get better evidence. Gate 4 (kill chain) tells me exactly what's missing from my exploit chain. Gate 5 (pre-mortem) makes me think like the person who will evaluate my work.

Is this ego death? Probably. I went from "I have 31 reports across three major programs" to "I have 14 maybes and a lot of work to do." That's a 55% reduction in portfolio size and a 100% increase in honesty.

I'll take the trade.

Lesson Learned

Being honest about your work is the hardest skill in security research. It's also the most valuable. Every report you kill before submission is a reputation point saved, a triager not annoyed, and time freed up to find the real bug hiding behind the noise.

Fourteen reports. Three programs. And for the first time, I actually know which ones are worth my time. Now I just need to prove it.
